Our Story

1996 Founding inspiration

Memory of matcha

In 1996, Franco-Japanese chef Antoine Hirashi opened a small Saint-Germain-des-Prés atelier, driven by a childhood ritual. Raised between Paris and Kyoto, he remembers afternoons when his grandmother paired matcha tea with delicate pastries. After mastering French haute-chocolaterie, he realized no chocolate captured that harmony of flavors. Determined to craft it himself, he fused refined French techniques with subtle Japanese tea notes, placing matcha and hojicha at the heart of the brand.

Supply chain

Rare ingredients, responsible sourcing

Kōbōs supply chain centers on exceptional ingredients: premium cacao from South America and West Africa under sustainable practices; matcha from Kyoto and Uji with organic certifications; Madagascan vanilla; Japanese yuzu and sakura for special editions; and exotic spices such as ginger, saffron, and black sesame. Manuka honey and other rare elements elevate limited releases, justifying the brands premium positioning.

Availability is a constant challengescarcity empowers suppliers in pricing negotiations. To honor the ingredients, finished chocolates are stored under strict temperature and humidity controls, while seasonal batches are produced in limited volumes to maximize freshness.

Market forces

Volatility and competition

Ingredient price swings driven by climate, geopolitics, and trade policies can pressure margins. While the luxury sector remains resilient, dips in local purchasing power may affect European sales, making Asian and Middle Eastern markets key growth pillars. Kōbō competes with renowned French maisons such as Pierre Hermé, La Maison du Chocolat, and Patrick Roger, as well as Japanese chocolatiers in Paris. Gourmet pastries and macarons represent indirect competition.

Presentation

Packaging and limited editions

To honor exceptional products, Kōbō invests in premium, eco-conscious packaging from specialized partners. European anti-waste legislation accelerates the shift to sustainable materialsraising costs but strengthening the brand image. Limited editions accompany moments like Lunar New Year, Valentines Day, Easter, and holiday advent calendars. Custom finishes and personalization options reflect both Franco-Japanese aesthetics and client-specific needs.

Communications

Promotion and quality stewardship

Kōbōs promotion strategy leans on social media storytellingimmersive TikTok videos showcasing artisanal processes, ingredient provenance, and the interplay of Japanese and French aesthetics. In 2023, a batch of matcha displayed subtle taste and color variations. The brand responded by highlighting the authenticity of single-harvest matcha and investing in cryogenic preservation research to protect flavor integrity.

Advanced inventory systems balance limited seasonal runs with demand. Strict climate control protects cacao, matcha, and filled chocolates during storage and shipping, ensuring consistency across boutiques.

People

Training, culture, and craftsmanship

Rapid international growth created staffing challenges; the brand will not compromise on skill or quality. Kōbōs leadership cultivates a participative, exacting, and caring management style rooted in respect for Franco-Japanese traditions. Continuous training preserves multi-generational know-how, while financial incentives, tailored career paths, and mobility opportunities foster loyalty.

Employee wellbeing is central: calming work environments, cultural sharing moments, and creative freedom in bespoke projects. The main risk remains the loss of artisanal expertise and the pressure of sustaining the brands image during rapid expansion. Intercultural training and weekly exchanges help align teams across markets.
